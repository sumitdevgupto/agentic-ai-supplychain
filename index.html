What Makes Agentic AI Different?

While traditional AI and generative AI require human prompts and focus on isolated recommendations, agentic AI operates with genuine autonomy. It identifies needs, plans multi-step solutions, executes actions, and continuously learns—all with limited human oversight.

Traditional AI

  • Requires human prompts
  • Provides recommendations
  • Focuses on single tasks
  • Waits for approval
  • Static response to queries

Agentic AI

  • Operates independently
  • Executes decisions autonomously
  • Orchestrates complex workflows
  • Acts in real-time
  • Learns and adapts continuously

The Four Phases of Agentic Development

Phase 1: First Insights

AI agents analyze structured and unstructured data within specific supply chain areas, delivering relevant information and suggesting courses of action through natural language queries.

Phase 2: Parallel Insights

Multiple specialized agents work simultaneously across different supply chain functions, providing comprehensive, coordinated analysis and recommendations.

Phase 3: Autonomous Execution

Agents execute decisions independently based on predefined parameters, from procurement to logistics adjustments, while maintaining human oversight for critical decisions.

Phase 4: Interconnected Networks

Fully autonomous, interconnected AI agents manage the entire supply chain ecosystem, negotiating with external partners and adapting to real-world events in real-time.

Source: SAP, "Agentic AI in the Global Supply Chain"

Key Success Factors

Start with robust data infrastructure: Companies like Walmart and P&G spent years consolidating supply chain data and building cloud-based machine learning pipelines before deploying agentic systems.

Define operational parameters clearly: Set explicit guidelines to ensure AI agents function within specified scopes. This prevents incorrect actions that could result in negative consequences.

Scale incrementally: DP World and the Port of Rotterdam adopted AI through hybrid approaches—internal innovation labs combined with external technology partnerships, testing pilot projects before scaling globally.

The Question Boards Should Be Asking

As supply chain professionals positioning ourselves for board and advisory roles, we need to shift the conversation from "Are we using AI?" to three harder questions:

1. Is our AI making decisions, or just highlighting them?
If humans are still the bottleneck between insight and action, you're optimizing yesterday's model.

2. Are we measuring time-to-action or time-to-insight?
The metric that matters isn't how fast you see the problem—it's how fast your system solves it.

3. What's our autonomous action threshold?
The companies pulling ahead have defined clear parameters: "The AI can act on anything under $X million impact without approval." What's your threshold? If the answer is zero, you're competing with one hand tied.

The Bottom Line

Agentic AI in supply chains isn't about replacing human judgment—it's about augmenting it with systems that can process billions of data points, execute decisions at machine speed, and continuously learn from outcomes.
